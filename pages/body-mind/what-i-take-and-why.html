What I Take and Why

A personal systems breakdown of every supplement and medication in my stack
~7 min read

I'm a systems guy. I can't help it. When something matters to me I want to understand the mechanism underneath it, not just follow the instructions on the bottle. My health is no exception.

For most of my adult life I didn't have a system here at all. I went years without a primary care doctor. I was in good shape when I left the fitness industry in 2024, but "good shape" was a vibe, not data. I had no idea what was actually happening under the hood. Then life compressed about a decade of change into eighteen months — marriage, three kids, a newborn, a career pivot, and a stint working overnight shifts that quietly wrecked my metabolic health before I understood what was happening.

Somewhere in there I finally started treating my body like every other system in my life: something to understand, measure, and optimize deliberately. What follows is the current state of that system. Not advice. Just what I take, and the actual reasoning behind each piece, because writing it down is how I think.

The Brain Stuff

Adderall XR 30mg + IR 15mg

I was diagnosed with ADHD in early 2026, and getting treated for it opened up things I'd spent my whole life white-knuckling through. The short version of how it works: ADHD brains run low on dopamine and norepinephrine in the prefrontal cortex — the part responsible for focus, impulse control, and the single hardest thing for me, starting tasks. Adderall increases the availability of those neurotransmitters so that region actually comes online.

The XR is a time-release version. About half the dose hits immediately and the other half releases a few hours later, which gives me a longer, smoother window than the immediate-release version does. I take it first thing in the morning, around 7:30.

I tracked my focus daily for a full month and the data was clear: the medication works well in the morning and falls off a cliff in the mid-afternoon, right around when my workday demands are still high and right before I get home to my family. So my prescriber and I added a small immediate-release dose at midday on work days to bridge that gap. The total still sits comfortably under the ceiling, and it targets the actual problem — duration — instead of just throwing a bigger morning dose at it.

One thing worth knowing if you're ever in a similar spot: I take an SSRI (more on that below), and it turns out that medication slows down how my body breaks down the Adderall. So my "30mg" is pharmacologically a little stronger than 30mg would be on its own. Small detail, but the kind of thing that matters when you're dialing in a dose.

Fluoxetine 20mg

This is an SSRI — it keeps more serotonin available in my brain, which helps with anxiety regulation. I started it during the most stressful stretch of my life, and unlike the Adderall, which I feel turn on and off, this one works quietly in the background and took weeks to build up.

It also connects to a couple of other things I take. Vitamin D supports the same serotonin pathway, so they reinforce each other. And the interaction with my ADHD meds I mentioned above means these two prescriptions are genuinely linked — something I make sure every doctor knows.

Hydroxyzine 25mg

An antihistamine that happens to be good at calming anxiety, taken in the evening. It's non-habit-forming, which is why I'd rather use it than the alternatives. Think of it as the bookend to the Fluoxetine — where that one runs all day in the background, this one shows up at night when the Adderall has worn off, the day's stress has piled up, and my nervous system needs to actually power down before sleep.

Alpha GPC 600mg (occasionally)

This one's a cholinergic — it boosts acetylcholine, which supports memory and sustained attention through a different pathway than the Adderall. I take it occasionally in the afternoon, never during the stimulant's peak, because stacking two cognitive boosters at once is a recipe for feeling wired and useless. Honestly, now that I have the midday IR dose, I'm reassessing whether I still need this at all.

The Heart and Metabolism Stuff

Pravastatin 10mg

A low-dose statin for cholesterol. It blocks the enzyme my body uses to manufacture cholesterol. Here's the detail most people miss: your body makes cholesterol mostly at night, so statins work best taken in the evening. I'm in the process of moving mine to my dinner lineup for exactly that reason.

The backstory here is the overnight job. When I finally got bloodwork done, my numbers were a mess — high cholesterol, high triglycerides, low HDL, the whole metabolic-syndrome picture. I've since learned that overnight shift work does this to people. It disrupts the body's clock in ways that wreck how you process fats and sugars. The statin was part of the response. Leaving that job was the bigger one.

Omega-3 Fish Oil (high dose)

This is the unsung hero of my cholesterol turnaround. My triglycerides peaked dangerously high, and the thing that actually brought them down — by nearly half — was high-dose fish oil, not the statin. Statins are great for one type of cholesterol; omega-3s specifically attack triglycerides by reducing how much your liver produces. I ran out about a month ago and I'm intentionally leaving it out until my next bloodwork, because the gap will tell me exactly how much work it was doing.

Magnesium Complex 500mg

Magnesium is involved in a few hundred reactions in the body, and most people running on high stress are low on it. I take it in the evening because it supports the wind-down — it helps activate the calming side of the nervous system, supports sleep, and has a modest blood pressure benefit, which matters to me given that stimulants nudge blood pressure up.

Vitamin D 5,000mg

Vitamin D acts more like a hormone than a vitamin — it influences mood, immune function, and cardiovascular health. I take a solid daily dose because, as I covered above, it backs up the same serotonin system my anxiety medication targets. It's also tied to testosterone and metabolic markers, both of which I'm keeping an eye on. I take it with dinner since it absorbs better alongside fat.

The Allergy and Breathing Stuff

Loratadine 10mg

A non-drowsy antihistamine for seasonal allergies, taken in the morning. I switched to this from a similar one that had a bit more of a sedating edge — better to keep the drowsy antihistamine (the Hydroxyzine) at night and the clean one in the daytime.

Fluticasone Nasal Spray

A steroid nasal spray for inflammation. This is the one I'm worst at being consistent with, and I know better. It only works if you use it every day — it builds up over time and falls off fast when you skip. Using it reactively gives me all of the hassle and none of the benefit. I have mild asthma, and nasal inflammation feeds into airway reactivity, so this isn't cosmetic. It's on my list to make automatic.

The Food-Based Stuff

I take three things with food that aren't pills, because the simplest interventions are often the ones with the most evidence behind them.

Chia seeds — about two tablespoons, for soluble fiber and a plant-based omega boost. Fiber binds up cholesterol in the gut and slows sugar absorption. Cheap, boring, effective.

Psyllium husk — same idea, even more evidence behind it specifically for cholesterol. I'm currently underdosing it at once or twice a week and I know daily is where the real benefit lives.

Creatine — the most-studied supplement on earth, mostly known for muscle but increasingly recognized for the brain too. I started it late last year. Consistency matters more than timing with this one.

The Real System

If I'm being honest with myself, the pills and powders are the easy part. The two levers that move my health the most aren't on this list: consistent sleep and regular movement. No supplement closes the gap that a wrecked sleep schedule or a sedentary week creates. I spent years in fitness — I know this better than most. The stack supports the foundation. It doesn't replace it.

This is a living system. It'll change with my next round of bloodwork, my next appointment, and whatever I learn between now and then. That's the point. You measure, you adjust, you measure again.

That's how I do everything else. Turns out it works for this too.

This is a personal log, not medical advice. Everything here is specific to me and decided alongside my doctors. If you're sorting out your own stack, talk to yours.
