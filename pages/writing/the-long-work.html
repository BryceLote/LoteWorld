THE LONG WORK

A Terraria Chronicle
Entry One · ~48 min read
Foundations

Every world begins as a question the land puts to whoever wakes inside it: will you only take from me, or will you make something here?
Most never answer. Bryce answered with his hands.

The forest did not know him yet.

That was the first thing he understood, standing at its edge in the thin gold of an early morning, with the dew still heavy enough to darken the toes of his boots. The trees leaned together the way trees do where no one has ever cut them, and the grass came up past his knees, and somewhere down the slope a small water made the patient sound that small waters make. It was a good place. He could see that the way some men can see, at a glance, whether a beam will hold weight. There was high ground for a house and low ground for a field, and stone underneath all of it, close to the surface where the roots had pried it loose. A person could live here. A person could do more than live.

He had come with almost nothing, which is how most people come to most places. A copper shortsword that had seen better owners. An axe. A pick whose edge he had already learned not to trust against anything harder than dirt. No coin to speak of. No plan he could have said aloud, only the old familiar pull he had felt his whole life, the one that turned every empty room into a list of things that ought to be built in it.

There was a man already there, sitting on a stump as though he had grown out of it. Older. Brown coat, kind eyes, the unbothered patience of someone who has watched a great many beginnings and is in no hurry to see how this one turns out.

"You'll want shelter before dark," the man said, by way of hello. "They come out at night. The dead, mostly. Things with one eye and no sense. The light keeps them honest." He said it the way a person reports the weather. Then, because Bryce had not asked: "I'm the Guide. People mean to learn my name and never do. You will not either, and I won't hold it against you."

Bryce nodded. He was already looking at the trees, and the slope, and the stone, and laying the whole thing out in his head the way he laid everything out: not as it was, but as it would be once he had spent enough patient hours on it. A door here. A wall there. A light against the dark.

"How long until dark," he asked. It was the only question that mattered yet.

The Guide squinted at the sun. "Long enough, if you don't waste it."

So Bryce did not waste it.

Chapter One

The First Night

He began with wood, because everything begins with wood.

The axe was poor but his arms were not, and by midday he had felled a stand of the tallest trees and dragged the logs to the high ground he'd chosen. He worked without hurry and without rest, which is a thing only certain people can do, the ones for whom the work is not the price of the reward but a quieter reward of its own. He liked the weight of the logs. He liked the clean smell where the axe opened them. He liked, most of all, the moment a pile of unrelated things became, under his hands, the first wall of something.

By the time the light began to lean and redden he had four walls and a roof and a door he had hung himself, crooked by a hair, and a single torch burning in a bracket he had set into the wood. It was not a house. It was the idea of a house, the smallest version of the promise. But it had an inside and an outside, and as the sun went down he understood, in his body, how much those two words were going to matter here.

The dark did not come gently. It came like a lid.

He heard them before he saw them. A wet, dragging shuffle in the grass he had walked through freely an hour before. Then the moans, low and stupid and somehow worse for being stupid, the sound of things that wanted him without quite knowing why. He stood at the small window he had left in the wall and watched the dead come up the slope, grey and slack and many, their arms working at the door he had hung. The wood shuddered. The crooked hinge held.

And above them, drifting, came the eyes.

He would never afterward be able to describe the eyes to anyone who had not seen them, the great floating things the size of a man's chest, lidless, the pupil swinging to fix on the light of his torch and then on him behind it. They did not shuffle. They glided, patient as he was patient, and that frightened him more than the teeth of the dead. He had the bow then, a crude thing of iron he had bent together at the rough bench in the corner, and a quiver of arrows he had wrapped in oiled cloth and set alight. He put the fire arrows through the window into the dark. He was not a marksman. He learned, that first night, by missing, and then by missing less.

It was a long night, and he did not sleep, and when the sky finally went grey in the east the survivors of the dark sizzled and came apart in the new light like frost off a window. He opened the crooked door and stepped out into a morning that smelled of wet ash and green growing things, and he stood there a moment with the bow still in his hand, and he felt the particular, private satisfaction of a man who has been tested by something and is still standing where he chose to stand.

The Guide was on his stump again, as if he had never moved.

"You kept the light," he observed.

"I kept the light," Bryce agreed.

Chapter Two

The Black Vein

He spent that second day going down.

He had seen the stone under the grass, and where there is stone there is everything else, and a man who means to build cannot build on what he can carry in from the surface alone. So he sank a shaft, neat and square, the walls of it true because he could not abide a crooked shaft even where no one would ever see it, and he climbed down into the close dark with a torch in his off hand and his poor pick in the other.

The underground was another country. The air was older down there, and it moved in slow drafts that meant rooms he had not found yet. There was copper in the walls, and tin, and deeper down a paler vein the Guide had named for him: silver, good for tools that would hold an edge. He mined it the way he did everything, methodically, leaving the walls square behind him, until he had enough to climb back up and make himself a real pick and a short blade of silver that did not turn when it met stone. He found iron too, and over the next descent and the next he beat it into a breastplate and greaves, and capped it with a helm of tin because he had not yet found iron enough for all of him. He did not mind the mismatch. A thing did not have to be finished to be useful. Most things never were.

And there were the gems. Topaz, mostly, gold-orange and warm to the eye in the torchlight, growing out of the stone in clusters like something the dark had dreamed. He gathered them without quite knowing why, beyond the plain fact that they were beautiful and he wanted to have them, which was reason enough. It was the Guide, back at the surface, who showed him the better reason: how a fistful of topaz, worked right, became a hook on a long bright line, a thing you could throw into a far ledge and trust to hold your weight.

The first time it held, and he swung out over a black drop he could not see the bottom of and landed clean on the far side, something opened in him that had been closed. The world had just gotten larger. He had just gotten harder to stop.

He ended that day at the mouth of a deeper cave, a throat of cold air breathing up out of the planet, and he stood at the edge of it with the new hook coiled at his hip and his mismatched armor on his back. He was better than he had been. He was not yet good enough, and he knew the difference, and knowing the difference was the whole of his particular wisdom. He did not go in. He marked it, the way he marked everything, and he climbed back up to his light, and he let the dark have its own for one more night.

The deeper he went, the less the world resembled anything he had words for.

Below the layer of common stone the caverns opened into spaces too large to light, where his torch threw his shadow huge against far walls and then lost it entirely in the dark beyond. He learned to read those spaces the way he had learned to read the forest. A certain echo meant water ahead. A faint, wrong sweetness in the air meant a pool of something that was not water. The slow blue drift of a spore meant he had wandered near a stranger country still, a cavern somewhere glowing with mushrooms the color of a bruise on a clear sky, and he filed that away, too, for later. There was always a later. He was building a map of laters, and the map was getting deep.

He traded up as he went, the way he always did, never leaping, only stepping. The topaz hook had taught him to want a better one, and when at last he found diamonds, hard and clear and cold even in the warm torchlight, he worked them into a new line that threw half again as far. With it the great vertical dark stopped being a wall and became a kind of road, and he went down it gladly. He finished the iron at last and put aside the tin helm and felt, briefly, the small completeness of a matched set, which pleased him more than it had any right to. He upgraded the bow again, silver now, truer. And in a quiet seam of the caverns he did a thing that no part of survival required of him: he planted a garden.

He had found, by then, how the world's logic ran. A seed and a gem, pressed together and set into bare stone, would take root in the rock itself and grow, slow and patient and crystalline, into a small hard tree that bore more gems than he had buried. He planted topaz and amethyst and a green flush of emerald, three little orchards in the underdark where nothing was meant to grow, and he tended them on his way past, and he told himself it was for the gems. It was partly for the gems. It was also because a man a long way down in the dark, alone, needs something to come back to that is alive and getting larger because he cared for it. He did not say this to the Guide. He did not say much to the Guide. He was, he would have admitted if anyone had pressed him and no one ever did, a little too good at being alone, the way a hand can grow too callused to feel what it holds.

He grew sturdier in those days, and not only in the ordinary way. Down in the oldest stone he began to find the hearts.

That was the only word for them. Crystalline, faceted, red-deep, beating their cold light out into the dark from where they had grown into the living rock, as though the planet kept its own pulse in places and he had stumbled onto one. The Guide had told him what to do and he had not believed it until he did it: he set his pick to one and broke it, and the light went out of it and into him, and something in his chest that he had never had cause to measure simply became larger. He could take more. He could fall farther and stand back up. He found a second such heart and broke that one too, and a strange band besides, a worn loop of metal that quickened the way his cuts and bruises closed, and he wore it without ceremony and was glad of it. He was harder to kill now than he had been. He noticed this the way he noticed everything, as a fact about a system, and he did not let himself enjoy it too much, because the part of him that kept him alive was the part that always assumed the world had something worse in reserve.

The world did. Of course it did.

He found the black vein on the fourth or fifth descent, he had stopped counting them, and he knew before his pick ever touched it that it was not ore the way the others were ore. It was dark with a purple drowned in the dark, and it drank his torchlight instead of throwing it back, and there was a hush around it, a held breath, the way a room goes quiet when something in it is listening. He set the silver pick to it the way he had set his pick to a thousand honest stones.

The pick slid off.

He tried again, harder, with the flat of his weight behind it, and it skated away again leaving the black vein unmarked and faintly, he could have sworn, amused. He crouched there a long while in the cold, turning the problem over the way he turned every problem, not with frustration, frustration was a waste, but with the slow circling patience of a man looking for the seam in a thing that does not want to be opened. There was always a seam. The world had not yet shown him a locked door he could not, given time, get behind.

He got behind this one sideways, which was the only way he ever got behind anything.

If the pick would not bite it, the pick was the wrong tool, and he had been a fool to think otherwise. He went back up. He found, at the edge of his small and growing settlement, a newcomer, a soot-stained man with a grin too wide for the news he carried and a satchel that smelled of cold powder, who would, for a fair price, part with the things that opened stone when stone refused to be reasoned with. Bryce bought what he could afford. He went back down. He packed the charges into the black vein with the care of a man who respects exactly how much he does not understand, and he backed off down the tunnel, and he brought the wall down.

When the dust cleared the black ore lay loose among the rubble, freed not by mastery but by ingenuity, which Bryce had long ago decided were close enough to the same thing for any practical purpose. He gathered it up, every dark fragment, and carried it back to his forge with a small private fire in his chest that had nothing to do with the explosion. He had wanted to make a pick of it. A pick that could bite anything. A key, really, to all the deeper doors he could already feel waiting under the ones he'd opened.

He came up one ingot short. He always came up one short of the thing he wanted most; he was beginning to suspect the world was built that way on purpose. But it was worse than short, and the Guide told him so, gently, the way you tell a careful man that his care will not be enough this time.

"That pick you're dreaming of," the Guide said, turning a fragment of the black ore in the firelight. "You can't forge it from this alone. It wants a second thing. Scales off the back of one of the old hungers, the ones that live out in the dark places of the world and were here long before you sank your first shaft." He set the fragment down. "There's no clever way around it, and I know how you like the clever way. Some doors you can pick. This one you have to go and earn. You'll have to find the thing, and face it, and take what it owes you."

Bryce said nothing for a while. He was a man who could blast through a wall the planet had locked, and he had just been told, plainly, that the next thing he wanted could not be blasted, or tunneled around, or out-waited, or out-built. It would have to be confronted. That was a different kind of work than the kind he was good at, and some honest part of him knew it, and filed that away too.

On his way back to the surface that night, he passed a seam he had not seen before, deep in the cavern wall where his torchlight only just reached: a thread of yellow, soft and unmistakable, running back into the rock farther than he could follow. Gold. Enough gold, if the vein ran true, to armor a man head to foot in something better than iron, to make him hard enough, maybe, to go and earn what could not be mined.

He marked it. He did not chase it. The dark was coming on above, and he had learned to respect the dark, and the gold would keep. Gold always kept. It was the patient ones, Bryce had begun to understand, who inherited the deep places of the world.

He climbed up toward his single faithful light, and behind him the caverns breathed their old cold breath, and far off to the west, past the edge of everything he had so far dared to know, something the color of the black vein was spreading slowly through the world like a stain through cloth, waiting, the way all hungers wait, to be found.

Chapter Three

East and West

He could not have the pickaxe. He could still have what the black ore wanted to be instead, and Bryce had never in his life let a thing go to waste because it would not be the thing he first imagined for it.

So he sat at his forge for the better part of a day and listened to the dark ore tell him what it was good for, the way he listened to all materials, and what it was good for, it turned out, was harm. It did not want to be a key. It wanted to be teeth. He drew it out into a bow that drank the light the way the vein had, and a short broad sword with a sick green edge that bit deeper than silver had any right to, and an axe with a head like a falling moon. When he was done he laid them out on the bench and looked at them for a while. They were good work. They were the best work he had done. And they were weapons, every one, which was not what he had set out to make of his life here, and he was honest enough with himself to feel the small strangeness of that. He had come to this place to build. The world kept handing him reasons to be armed.

He had been going down for so many days that he had half forgotten the surface had a width as well as a depth. Now, armed, he went to find out.

He walked east first, because the light came from there and a man instinctively walks toward the light. The forest thinned and yellowed and then gave up entirely, and the ground went to sand, and the sand went on. The desert was a country with no patience in it, all heat and glare and the dry clatter of things living just under the surface, antlions that erupted from the dunes and spat at him before he understood what was happening. He learned to watch the sand for the wrong kind of stillness. He crossed it the way he crossed everything, steadily, and on the far side the world changed its mind entirely and became a riot.

The jungle did not want him there and made no secret of it. It was wet and close and almost violently alive, green stacked on green stacked on green, and everything in it had a sting or a tooth. Hornets came at him out of the canopy. Things he never got a clear look at moved in the undergrowth. He loved it, in the wary way he loved any system dense enough to take real study, and he took what it offered him, vines and strange spores and a flail he found half buried in a ruined shrine, a heavy thing of spiked iron that he wrapped in fire so that when he swung it the close dark filled with arcs of burning. That one he kept close. The caves under his settlement were crowded places, and a man who can fill the crowded dark with swinging fire is a man who walks through it unafraid.

He found other gifts as he went, the way a careful walker always does. Boots that, when he first dared to run flat-out in them, carried him so fast the wind tore at his eyes, so that the long traverses that had cost him whole afternoons became a thing he could do between breakfast and the heat of the day. Claws he could fix to his hands to cling to a sheer wall and climb it like a thing born to it. Arrows that did not stop at the first body but passed through and through, three and four enemies on a single flight. And smaller things, a band that quickened the cold spark of magic he had not yet learned to use, trinkets and charms whose purposes he half understood, statues of forgotten kings, paintings of places that might not exist anymore. He hauled all of it home. He could not help it. A full pack pleased him the way a full chest pleased him, the way the orchards of gems pleased him, not for what he would do with it but for the plain having of it, the slow accumulation that was, he had begun to suspect, the truest shape of his happiness. He was not building toward a finish. He was building because the building was the life.

It was somewhere in the jungle, or maybe the deep desert, that he found the eyes.

There were two of them, and they were not like anything else he had pocketed. Each was the size of his fist, lidless, the iris a sick and watchful red, warm in his palm as though something still lived in it, and when he held one up to the light the pupil seemed, slowly, to turn and find him. He should have thrown them into the sea. Instead he wrapped them in cloth and put them deep in his pack with the statues and the strange charms, because they were the most uncanny things he had ever found and the magpie in him could not let them go. He did not know what they were. He told himself they were curiosities. He did not connect them, not yet, to the great floating thing that had drifted up the slope at him on his first night in the world, the night he had kept the light. The world is patient about its connections. It lets you carry the answer in your pack for days before it asks you the question.

When he turned and went west, the world stopped being merely dangerous and started being wrong.

He smelled it before he saw it, that faint wrong sweetness he had met once underground and never forgotten. Then the grass under his boots went grey, and then a bruised purple, and the trees grew strange and reaching, and the ground itself opened in long chasms that dropped away into a dark that breathed. Things drifted through the murk that were nothing but mouths, slow and certain, eaters of something he did not want to name. There was a held-breath silence over all of it, the silence of the black vein magnified across a whole country, and at the bottom of one chasm he saw an altar of dark stone that made the hair stand up on his arms from forty feet away. This was the stain he had glimpsed from the deep caverns. It had a name; the Guide gave it to him later, quietly, the way you name a sickness. The Corruption. It was spreading, the Guide said. It had always been spreading. It would have to be reckoned with, in time, by someone, and the Guide did not say by whom but he looked at Bryce when he did not say it.

Bryce did not go deep. For once he did not even want to. He read the wrongness the way he read everything, took its measure, marked its edges, and withdrew. There was a kind of courage in pushing into a place like that, and there was a kind of foolishness, and the whole of Bryce's particular wisdom had always been knowing which was which. He would come back. Not today.

And then, because the world is never only one thing, he found beauty right beside the wrongness. A little way from the grey country a cavern glowed, soft and blue and impossible, lit from within by mushrooms the color of a clear evening sky, the bruise-colored spores he had glimpsed weeks ago made into a whole quiet country of light. He stood in it a long while. And half buried in that glowing place he found an old machine, a squat and patient thing that, when he fed it the worthless rubble of his diggings, sifted and shook and gave back small treasures, coins and gems and stranger ore, drawing the valuable out of the worthless by some logic he did not need to understand to love. He carried it home on his back, all that long way, and set it up beside his forge, and it pleased him more than the fire-flail or the moon-headed axe, because it was not a weapon. It made something out of nothing. It was, in its small way, the thing he most wanted to be.

He tried magic, once, in those days. He had the spark for it, the quickened cold the band had given him, and he found a way to bind a ruby into a staff, and he expected, the way you expect when you have done the work, to be rewarded with power. The staff threw a red bolt that was barely better than the one his cheap topaz could have thrown, for twice the cost to use it, and Bryce stood there in his settlement turning it over with the faint specific disappointment of a man who has learned, again, that the world does not pay out in proportion to the effort you put in. He did not throw the staff away. He filed the lesson where he filed all his lessons, and he set the staff aside, and he let magic be a door for another day. There would be a magic that answered him. This was not it. He was patient about doors.

He had learned the shape of his world, now. Life and edges to the east. Wrongness and beauty to the west. Patient riches and old hungers below. And himself in the middle of it, armed with teeth he would rather not have needed, rich in things he could not use, one threshold still short of ready. The gold was still down there, waiting in its seam. He had marked it weeks ago. It was time, he thought, to go and become hard enough to be owed something by the dark.

Chapter Four

Gold

The vein ran true, which is the most a man can ask of any promise.

He went down to it with the wind-boots on his feet and the fire-flail at his hip and a hunger for the work that was almost happiness, and he gave himself to the gold the way he gave himself to all the best labor, without hurry, without rest, the square tunnels opening true behind him into the deep stone. The yellow thread he had glimpsed thickened as he chased it, branched, opened into a body of gold richer than he had let himself hope. He hauled it up load by load. He did not count the trips. Counting was for men who resented the work, and Bryce had never resented the work in his life.

When he had enough he forged the full set, head to foot, and put it on, and stood in the firelight of his settlement feeling the matched weight of it settle onto his shoulders. He had worn iron, and tin, and mismatched scraps, and made do, because a thing did not have to be finished to be useful. But there is a particular completeness to a thing finally finished, and he let himself have it, this once, the rare clean pleasure of standing armored properly for the first time since he had come to this place with a poor pick and no plan. He was not a scavenger in borrowed pieces anymore. He was something the world would have to take seriously.

And in the deep run for the gold, he found the hearts in numbers.

He had broken two before and felt himself enlarge. Now the old stone gave them up to him by the handful, ten of the cold faceted things beating their red light out into the dark, and he broke every one, and each time the thing in his chest that measured how much harm he could take simply grew, and grew, and kept growing, until he crossed some line inside himself he had not known was there. He had come to this world a man who could be ended by a single bad night, by one unlucky press of the dead against a crooked door. He was not that man anymore. He could fall farther now, bleed longer, wade into worse and come back out. He noticed the change the way he noticed all changes, as a plain fact about a system, and he did not let himself glory in it, because the part of him that had kept him alive this long was the part that always assumed the world had something worse in reserve. But he let himself feel it, a little. He had become hard to kill. A man is allowed to feel that.

The deep had its own surprises for him, the way it always did. He broke through a wall expecting more of the common stone and found instead a hall of black glass, smooth and angular and far too regular to be anything the world had made by accident, the walls cut into facets that threw his torchlight back at him in a hundred cold splinters. He had a moment to admire it. Then the walls unfolded. The same black glassy stone stood up off the floor in the shape of men with no eyes and came at him swinging fists like cut boulders, and he met them with the fire-flail and the green-edged sword, and it was a hard close fight in a beautiful terrible room, and he won it because he was patient even in a fight, even afraid, picking his moments, never overcommitting. When it was done he stood among the broken golems breathing hard and looked at the cold glittering hall and understood that the world had more kinds of strangeness in it than he would live to catalogue. He took the measure of the place and moved on. Even his hunger for laters had its limits.

It met its limit, that day, at the spider nest.

He found it the way you find the worst rooms, by the dread that arrives before the sight does. The webbing came first, thickening on the walls of the tunnel until the stone disappeared under it, grey and dense and faintly moving. Then the cavern opened, and it was wrapped throat to floor in the stuff, and at the edges of his torchlight things skittered that he could not quite see and did not want to, fast and low and many. There was treasure in a place like that. He knew it the way he knew where beams would hold. The worst rooms always kept the best things; the world charged for its treasures in exactly the coin a man was least willing to pay. Every instinct he had built across these long weeks, the instinct that said go in, gather, the having is the happiness, leaned him toward the dark.

He stood at the edge of it a long moment. And he turned around.

He was not ashamed of it, which was its own kind of strength. A lesser version of himself, or a younger one, might have pushed in to prove something to no one, and died in the webbing for a chest he could have come back for when he was ready and unafraid. The whole of his wisdom had always been knowing the difference between the brave thing and the foolish thing, and the harder, quieter half of that wisdom was knowing when not to do something he was perfectly capable of doing. He left the nest to its skittering dark. He was honest with himself, walking away, that it had unnerved him, and he did not dress the retreat up as strategy. It was strategy. It was also fear, and a man who cannot admit the second will eventually get killed pretending it was only the first.

He let the glowing mushroom country, when he passed back through it, be a kind of apology the world made to him for the spiders, all that soft impossible blue light, and he breathed it in, and then he climbed for home.

He came up into the evening a different creature than the one who had gone down. Full gold on his back, and the long red weight of the hearts inside him, and the demon bow and the green sword and the fire that swung. He had become, without quite meaning to, formidable. And in the bottom of his pack, when he set it down by the fire that night, his hand found the two wrapped eyes, still warm, still watchful, and at last the connection the world had been patient about made itself in his mind all at once, the way the best and worst understandings always do.

He knew what they were for. Or the Guide told him, looking at the things in Bryce's palm with an expression Bryce had not seen on him before. They were a summons. They were how a man called the great drifting horror down out of the night on purpose, the grown and dreadful kin of the eye that had glided up the slope at him on his very first night in the world. And the thing he wanted, the locked door, the key he could not forge, waited on the far side of facing one of the old hungers and taking what it owed. He was holding, in his own hand, the means to call the first of them.

He sat with that for a long time. He was a man who built things, who would always rather make a wall than break a thing against it, who had blasted through a locked vein and tunneled around every obstacle the world had put in front of him by being patient and being clever and finding the seam. And here at last was a thing with no seam. A door that would not open to patience or cleverness or a full pack. It would open only to a man willing to walk out under the open moon and meet a horror on purpose, and stand his ground, and not run.

He turned the warm eye over in his fingers and watched the pupil find him in the firelight. Overhead the moon came up full and white over the little settlement he had made out of nothing, the walls and the orchards and the patient machine that drew treasure out of rubble, the whole small proof that he could make a place safe and good through steady work.

Steady work would not be enough this time. He had known it since the Guide first told him so, down by the forge, with the black ore cooling between them. Some doors you have to go and earn.

Tomorrow night, he decided, he would go and earn it.

He did not sleep much. He was not afraid, exactly. He was getting ready, in the only way he had ever known how, which was to lay the whole thing out in his head as it would be, and to mean it.

Chapter Five

The Old Hungers

He spent the last day before it readying, because readying was the only prayer he knew.

He widened the house into something that deserved the name, more rooms than one man needed, and he could not have said why he built them except that a place with empty rooms in it is a place expecting company, and some part of him had begun, without permission, to expect company. He brewed what the Dryad's herbs and the orchard's strange flowers would give him, draughts that quickened healing and toughened skin and lent the feet a borrowed swiftness. He laid arrows by the hundred. He cleared a long flat stretch of ground east of the house and ringed it with torches, a floor to fight on, lines of sight he had chosen on purpose. He was a man who could not control what was coming, so he controlled everything around it, which is what careful people do with their fear.

And the day before, digging out a low chamber, he had found the egg.

It was large and pale and faintly cold to the touch, and he had brought it up into the warmth by the fire more out of curiosity than hope, and in the night it had cracked, and a small thing had come out of it. A dragon, or the idea of one, scaled and bright-eyed and no bigger than a dog, that breathed a thin curl of frost when it yawned and looked up at Bryce with the total uncomplicated trust of a creature that has decided, instantly and forever, whose it is. He named it nothing for a while, the way he named nothing, and it did not care, and it followed him, and when he ran flat-out in the wind-boots it bounded and then glided alongside him, and a man who had been a long time alone in a hard country found that he did not mind, at all, having something at his heel that was glad he existed. He would carry the little cold dragon into the worst of what came next. It turned out he could not imagine, anymore, going without it.

When the moon came up full he went out to the torchlit floor he had made, and he took one of the warm watchful eyes out of his pack, and he crushed it in his fist.

The sky answered.

It came down out of the dark the way it had on his very first night, the great drifting horror, only grown now past all reason, a single vast eye trailing a fringe of its own wet flesh, fixing him with a pupil the size of a door. It was the thing that had taught him fear in this world. He had spent every day since becoming a man it would have to take seriously, and now here it was, and he found, standing under it with the cold dragon hissing at his heel and the demon bow in his hands, that he was afraid and steady at the same time, which he had learned was the only honest kind of brave.

Then it charged, and there was no more time for thinking, only for the thing he had practiced in his head a hundred times. He ran. The wind-boots tore the ground out from under him and the demon arrows went up into that drifting bulk one after another after another, and the dragon's frost laced the dark, and the eye screamed and bled and birthed smaller horrors out of itself to harry him, and he kept moving, kept loosing, never planting his feet too long, never overcommitting, picking it apart the patient way he picked apart everything. And when it could bleed no more it came at him all at once in a final blind rush, and he stood his ground for that, because some moments you cannot sidestep, and he put the last arrows straight down its ruined eye as it came, and it broke apart above him and rained down and was gone.

He stood a while in the quiet afterward, breathing, the dragon winding around his ankles. The thing that had terrified him on night one was a litter of fading scraps on the floor he had built. He did not feel triumphant. He felt, mostly, the strange flat calm of a man who has crossed a line he spent a long time walking toward, and finds the far side is just more ground.

But the eye had not been the door. The eye had only been the proof that he could stand in front of an old hunger and not run. The door was west, in the grey country he had withdrawn from, and now he went back into it on purpose.

He went down into one of the breathing chasms, the cold dragon pressed close, past the altar of dark stone, into the deep of the Corruption, and there in the murk he found them: orbs of dark glass set into the walls, glowing with a sick inner light, the way the heart-crystals had glowed but wrong, inverted. He broke the first one because breaking things to see what they held was the truest reflex he had, and it gave up to him, among other things, a staff.

He knew it was different the moment he held it. He had tried magic once and been disappointed, and filed the lesson away, and told himself there would be a magic that answered him and this was not it. This was it. When he leveled the dark staff a spectral thorn tore out of it and through the earth itself, through stone and through the bodies of the drifting mouths, a green-white lance of pure intent that did not care what stood between him and what he meant to hit. It answered him. It moved the way his own mind moved, in a straight line through whatever was in the way. He stood in the wrong glowing dark and loosed it again and again for the plain joy of a tool that finally fit his hand, and he understood that the world had made him wait for this on purpose, the way it made him wait for everything, so that he would know it when it came.

He broke the second orb, and the third, and that was the thing the grey country had been waiting for.

The ground heaved. Something came up out of the deep of the chasm that had been there, he understood at once, long before he had ever sunk a shaft, the old hunger of the Corruption itself, a worm vast beyond the torchlight, segment after armored segment of it boring up through the earth with a sound like the world grinding its teeth. This was the one the Guide had named. This was the keeper of the thing he had come to earn. It did not drift like the eye. It came through the solid stone as though stone were water, and there was nowhere to run that it could not follow, and so Bryce did not try to run from it. He turned and fought it in the close dark, the spectral thorn tearing down its length, the demon arrows finding the gaps in its armor, the dragon's frost, the green sword when it came near enough to bite. It was long and it was patient and so was he, and it broke apart a segment at a time, each piece thrashing and dying on its own, until the last of it shuddered and went still and the chasm was quiet again but for his own hard breathing and the small cold huff of the dragon.

The old hunger had owed him something, and now it paid. Among the ruin of it lay the scales, dark and supple and faintly warm, shadow given a skin. He gathered every one.

He climbed up out of the grey country and went to his forge and did, at last, the thing he had wanted to do since the night his pick first slid off the black vein. He took the demonite he had blasted from the walls and the shadow the old hunger had owed him, and he forged the pickaxe. The key. When he set it to the deepest, hardest, most stubborn stone he had ever cursed at, it bit, clean and eager, and a whole layer of the world that had been locked against him fell open in his mind, every deep door he had marked and left, suddenly his. And from the rest of the dark scales he made armor, close and light and black, that lent his feet a swiftness all their own, so that he moved now like something the dark had made and then lost control of.

He had earned it. Not built it, not blasted it, not out-waited it. Earned it, by walking out to meet two horrors and standing his ground. It was a different kind of work than the kind he was good at, and he had done it, and it had changed him a little, the way the hearts had changed him, in a place he had not known he could grow.

And while he had been gone, becoming that, the empty rooms of his house had begun to fill.

They came the way people come to anywhere that has proven it can keep the dark out: quietly, and then all at once. A painter with sad eyes and a cart of colors. A boy who did nothing but fish and complain about it. A woman who studied every beast that wandered near and wrote it all down. And one who was not quite a woman, green and old and rooted, who knew the grey country better than he did and looked at his black new armor with something between approval and grief. The man who had been too good at being alone came home from earning his terrible key to find his table louder than he had left it, and he sat down at it, and he was, to his own quiet surprise, glad.

Chapter Six

A People

The old hunger of the grey country, it turned out, would come back if he called it, and pay again.

He went down into the Corruption a second time and a third, almost easy with it now, the spectral thorn and the dark armor making short work of what had nearly killed him days before, and he took more scales, because a careful man does not stop at barely enough of a good thing. There is a particular grim comfort in that, the moment a horror becomes a resource, a thing you visit on a schedule. He did not let it make him careless. But he let it make him confident, which is different, and the difference was most of what he had come to this world to learn.

Then he went to the jungle, for the matriarch.

He found her the way you find the worst and best things, deep in the green riot, in a vaulted hive thick with the smell of honey and venom, and he woke her, and she came at him enormous and furious, all stinger and wing and the screaming of her brood. It was a hard fight in a hot close place, and he gave it everything, the thorn and the bow and the fire-flail, the cold dragon darting, and when at last she fell she left him, among other things, a weapon he loved the moment he closed his hand on it. A keeper's tool, a thing that loosed a swarm of his own at whatever he struck, so that he was never again alone in a fight, always trailing a small furious cloud that fought for him. Of all the things he had made and earned and gathered, this was the one he kept reaching for, the way a man keeps reaching for the tool that simply feels like his hand. He could not have said why. He did not need to. A person is allowed to love a thing for no reason he can put into words.

And then the world, for the first time, came to him.

He was in the settlement when the horns sounded, and over the eastern rise came a horde, a goblin army, ragged and many and bent on the thing the dead and the eyes had wanted from the start, which was simply to unmake the lit and ordered place a man had made in the dark. He had built the floor to fight on. He had built the rooms and the walls and the lines of sight. Now he found out what they were for. He held the line at the edge of his own town with the thorn tearing through their ranks and the bees swarming and the dragon's frost and the long patient discipline of a man who does not panic, and the neighbors he had barely met sheltered behind him, and he kept the light. When it was over and the horde was broken on the ground he had chosen, he found among the survivors a prisoner the goblins had dragged along in chains, one of their own who had turned tinkerer, a sour clever creature with a genius in his hands, and Bryce struck the chains off him and gave him a room.

It was the best thing he recruited that day, though he did not know it yet. The tinkerer could take the trinkets Bryce had hoarded and could not fully use, the wind-boots and the climbing claws and the bands and charms, and fuse them, two and three at a time, into single greater things, so that all that magpie accumulation finally became power: boots that ran like wind and leapt like lightning, gear that let him scale and cling and vault as one motion, a band that fed his new deep magic faster than it drained. The full pack that had pleased Bryce only for the having of it now pleased him for what the tinkerer made it into. The collecting had been the foundation. This was the building it had always been for.

A red moon rose not long after, and turned the night the color of an open wound, and the dead came again in their numbers the way they had on his very first night in the world. He stood out in it and held his light, and it was not even close, this time, and somewhere in the middle of that long red night he understood how far he had come without ever once feeling himself arrive. The man who had learned to shoot by missing now turned away a Blood Moon as a matter of course, and went to bed when it was done.

The town kept filling. They had a way of finding a proven place. A dealer in guns and a keeper of a tavern from some far-off war that had nothing to do with this country. A wanderer of bones who walked in only at dusk and out by dawn, selling oddments, among them a strange dense loaf that, eaten, seemed to lengthen a craftsman's very reach, so that Bryce could work from farther off and waste fewer steps, a small thing that pleased the efficient soul of him absurdly. A trader in dyes. And, drawn by the death of the matriarch in the jungle, a healer and a hexer both, a witch of the green country whose name was the first name Bryce had bothered to learn since the Guide told him, all those weeks ago, that he never would. Kohna. He learned it on purpose. He noticed himself doing it, and did not examine why too closely, except that a man does not learn the name of someone he expects to leave.

He gave them places to live, because that was the work he understood best of all. He spaced their houses so the sour tinkerer did not have to smell the dye trader's vats, and put the ones who liked each other near, and the ones who didn't, far, the way you'd lay out anything you meant to last. He learned what they sold and what they wanted and who they were, slowly, in his sidelong self-contained way. And he built them roads.

That was the last great work of those days, and the one most purely his. He raised a long stone platform running east, true and level, all the way to the edge of the world, with lines dropped from it so a man could climb up or down to any depth as he passed, so that the traverse that had once cost him afternoons cost him minutes now, and then he set the standing stones, the humming pylons, one in the heart of his town and one in the deep jungle, paired so that a man might step into the air beside one and out of the air beside the other, and the far places of the world folded suddenly near. He stood back and looked at the road and the stones the way he had looked, on his first morning, at an empty slope, seeing the thing not as it was but as it would be, except that now the seeing and the thing were the same, because he had made it so.

He had come to this place alone, with a poor pick and no plan, to build something. He turned around in the lit and humming heart of it on a quiet night, the pickaxe at his hip that could bite anything the world had, the dark armor on his back, the small cold dragon asleep by the fire, the strange loud beloved household breathing in their rooms all around him, and he understood that he had built more than he set out to. He had meant to make a place safe. He had made a people, and the people had, without his ever quite noticing the moment it happened, made him one of them. The hand that had grown too callused to feel what it held was, it seemed, still capable of feeling this.

It should have felt like an ending. It did not. He stood at the western edge of his town that night and looked the one direction he had not built, where the grey country still spread, slow and patient, the way all hungers spread, and beyond it the land went white and cold into a snow he had not crossed, and somewhere out there in the dark of the world, on the side away from the jungle, stood a great locked structure he had glimpsed only once and not entered, guarded, the Guide said, by an old thing that did not die easily. And below all of it the world dropped away toward a deep he had only just become able to reach, toward fire, toward a wall of it, that a man could break if he were strong enough and foolish enough, and that, broken, would change the whole world at once and forever, and there would be no building it back the way it had been.

He did not know all of that yet. He felt the shape of it, the way he felt the shape of everything before he understood it, the sense that all of this, the house and the roads and the pickaxe and the people, every hard-won thing, was foundation. That the long work had not ended. That it had, in truth, only just laid down enough beneath itself to begin.

He turned the new pickaxe over in his hand, and watched the moon go down over the unbuilt west, and he was not afraid, exactly.

He was getting ready.
