The One Thing I Didn't Build

A response to the sermon preached by Pastor Woody
Family Church Yukon · 9:15 AM · Sunday, May 31, 2026

I have always built the thing I needed instead of waiting for someone to hand it to me. When I wanted to understand computers, I took them apart on the floor until they made sense. When I wanted a career, I built one out of operations and sales and long hours and a refusal to quit. When I wanted a family, I built that too, deliberately, the way you build anything you intend to outlast you. I trust my own hands more than I trust luck, and almost everything I have is evidence that the instinct was right.

So it took me a long time to notice the one thing I kept reaching for and could never quite build.

I didn't have words for it until this morning, in a small church, when a woman stood up to lead worship and, before she meant to, preached.

· · ·

She read one verse out of Ephesians and then asked the room a question she clearly hadn't scripted. The verse was the one about grace. You are saved by grace, through faith, and this is not from yourselves; it is a gift, not a reward, so that no one can boast. I have heard it a hundred times. I have probably quoted it to someone else. But I heard it differently sitting there, partly because it is the verse my wife and I chose for our daughter Gianna when she was born, and partly because the entire morning turned out to be about the one thing I am worst at receiving.

Then she asked where Jesus sits. On the throne, or just beside it. Is he actually the king of your life, or does he take a back seat to your circumstances. It is an easy question to nod along to and a hard one to answer honestly, because most of us, if we are paying attention, know exactly where the throne is, and it is usually occupied.

I know where mine was. For a long stretch of my late teens I sat on it alone, and I ran my own life directly into the ground. I was certain I knew more about how to live than anyone trying to tell me otherwise. I wanted independence more than I wanted to be well. By eighteen I had proven, thoroughly, that I could destroy things faster than anyone could repair them, myself included. That is the part of the story I tend to move past quickly, not because I am ashamed of it anymore, but because it is the least interesting thing about me now. What matters is that being my own king was not freedom. It was the worst government I have ever lived under.

· · ·

The pastor preached out of Matthew that morning, and his whole message hung on one word that most of us have quietly defanged. Repentance. We have made it mean being sorry, the way we tell someone we didn't mean to do the thing we did. He pulled it back to its real shape. Repentance is not an apology. It is a change of mind that produces a change of action. It is the difference between editing your behavior and rebuilding your beliefs. He said, and I think this is the truest thing anyone has ever told me about change, that if you only change the action and never touch the mind underneath it, the change will not hold. It comes back. You will repent of the same thing over and over because you never actually crossed the table and admitted you were wrong about what you believed.

I know he is right because I have the proof in my own life.

When I finally got off the throne, I didn't just stop the behavior. If I had only stopped the behavior, I would have started again. What happened instead is that I left for an island and spent the better part of a decade becoming a different person than the one who had been sitting there. Seven years. The actions changed because the man underneath them changed first, and that is the only reason any of it held. It has now held through a marriage, through becoming a father, through every hard season since. Nothing white-knuckled survives that long. Only a changed mind does. I was living out a verse about the renewing of the mind years before I could have told you where to find it.

There is a version of repentance the church doesn't talk about as much, and the pastor named it too. We are comfortable confessing to God in private, where it costs us nothing in front of anyone. We are far less comfortable saying the true thing out loud to another human being. But that, he said, is where the healing actually happens. A thing kept in the dark stays powerful. A thing brought into the light loses its grip. I have found this to be exactly true. Every burden I have ever carried got lighter the moment I stopped managing it alone and let someone else see it. The relief was never in being forgiven. It was in being known.

· · ·

He said one more thing that I have not been able to put down. Prove it by the way you live. Not by the moment you raised your hand at the front of a church. Not by the decision you can point back to. By the life. A testimony is not a sentence; it is a direction you keep walking. God is enough, he said, but Sunday never will be.

That landed because the proof of everything I believe is not a moment I can name. It is a life I had to build. And here is where the morning finally closed the loop I had been carrying into the building without knowing it.

I am a builder. I earned the career. I earned the body of work. I even earned, in the sense that I had to become someone first, the right to be the husband and father I am now. My own father understands this in a way few people do, because he was chosen. His biological father was absent, and my grandfather stepped in and adopted him, made him a son not by blood but by decision. That act got handed down. I carried it forward when I adopted two of my children, choosing them the way my grandfather chose my dad. Three generations of belonging that nobody earned and somebody simply gave.

That is the one thing I could never build. I kept trying to earn my way to solid ground, to a sense that I was finally enough, finally square with the ledger, finally safe. And the verse I have quoted a hundred times, the verse on my daughter's birth, says plainly that it does not work that way and was never supposed to. Grace is not a reward for the house you built. It is the ground the house is standing on. You cannot pour it. You cannot earn it. You can only notice that it was holding you up the entire time you thought you were holding up yourself.

I have built almost everything in my life with my own hands, and I am not sorry for any of it. But the one thing I didn't build turned out to be the only thing underneath all of it that I could not have lived without. I spent years trying to construct the foundation. It had already been given to me. I just had to come to my senses, get off the throne, and let it hold.
