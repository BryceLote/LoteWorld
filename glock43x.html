01 Overview & History

Right Side

Standard model with silver nPVD slide finish, Slimline frame, and factory polymer sights. 3.41" barrel, 6.50" overall length.

Left Side

Ambidextrous slide stop lever, reversible magazine catch, and front slide serrations visible from this angle.

The Glock 43X is a 9mm Luger semi-automatic pistol released in July 2019 as part of Glock's Slimline series. It occupies a hybrid position between the ultra-compact Glock 43 (6-round capacity) and the slightly longer Glock 48, combining the 43's short slide with a full-length grip frame that accommodates a 10-round flush-fit magazine. The result is a pistol specifically optimized for concealed carry that provides substantially better capacity and shootability than the original G43 without significantly increasing its footprint.

As a Gen 5 platform, the 43X incorporates several design improvements over earlier generations: an ambidextrous slide stop lever, a flared magazine well for faster reloads, the absence of finger grooves on the grip (allowing a more universal fit across hand sizes), and the Glock Marksman Barrel (GMB) with improved rifling for better accuracy.

The 43X quickly became one of the most popular concealed carry pistols in the United States. In 2020, Glock expanded the lineup with the 43X MOS (Modular Optic System), which features a factory-milled slide to accept micro red dot optics. The MOS version uses the RMSc footprint and, as of 2025, offers direct mounting for Holosun 407K/507K/EPS, Vortex, and Aimpoint optics without adapter plates.

03 How the Glock 43X Works

The Safe Action System

Safe Action Overview

The three-safety system. Trigger safety (face lever), firing pin safety (plunger), and drop safety (trigger bar) work independently.

Trigger Mechanism Anatomy

Trigger bar, connector, and cruciform. Pulling the trigger lifts the firing pin safety plunger and releases the partially pre-loaded striker.

Unlike firearms with a manual safety lever, the Glock 43X relies on Glock's Safe Action system. It is a fully automatic, passive safety mechanism consisting of three independent mechanical safeties that engage and disengage without any conscious input from the shooter. All three safeties disengage sequentially as the trigger is pulled and re-engage automatically when the trigger is released.

Trigger Safety. A small lever integrated into the face of the trigger. It mechanically blocks rearward trigger movement unless the lever itself is deliberately depressed by the shooter's finger. This prevents discharge from lateral pressure, drops, or brushing against objects in a holster.
Firing Pin Safety. A spring-loaded plunger that physically blocks the firing pin channel. As the trigger is pulled rearward, the trigger bar pushes this plunger upward, clearing the channel. If the trigger is not pulled, the firing pin cannot move forward to strike the primer, even under severe impact.
Drop Safety. The trigger bar itself acts as the third safety. It prevents the firing pin from being released unless the trigger bar is in the fully rearward position, which only occurs during a deliberate trigger pull. This ensures the pistol will not fire if dropped or jarred.

The practical effect: if the trigger is forward, all safeties are engaged. Pulling the trigger disengages all three in sequence and fires the round. Releasing the trigger re-engages all three. There is no safety switch to forget.

Firing Cycle

The Glock 43X operates on a short-recoil, locked-breech system with a modified Browning cam-lock action.

A round is chambered from the magazine by the slide moving forward under spring tension. The barrel and slide lock together via the barrel's locking block engaging the ejection port. Pulling the trigger releases the partially pre-loaded striker, which travels forward under spring tension, strikes the primer, and ignites the cartridge.

As the bullet travels down the barrel, rearward pressure pushes the barrel and slide backward together. After a short distance, the barrel's cam surfaces cause it to tilt downward and unlock from the slide. The slide continues rearward alone, extracting and ejecting the spent casing while compressing the recoil spring.

The recoil spring drives the slide forward, stripping a fresh round from the magazine and chambering it. The barrel tilts upward and locks into the slide again. The trigger resets with a short forward movement (audible/tactile click), and the pistol is ready to fire again.

Trigger Characteristics

The 43X trigger has a pull weight of approximately 5.5 to 6.5 pounds. The trigger stroke includes a short take-up (pre-travel), a defined "wall" where resistance increases, and a clean break. After firing, the trigger resets with a short forward movement and a tactile/audible click. This short reset allows for quick follow-up shots without fully releasing the trigger.

04 Basic Operation

Loading

Confirm the pistol is pointed in a safe direction. Insert a loaded magazine into the grip until it clicks and locks in place. Grasp the rear serrations of the slide firmly and rack it fully rearward, then release. Do not ride the slide forward. Let the recoil spring do the work. This chambers a round. You now have a round in the chamber plus remaining rounds in the magazine (e.g., 10+1 with a factory magazine).

Unloading

Point in a safe direction. Remove the magazine by pressing the magazine release button. Rack the slide fully rearward to eject the chambered round. Lock the slide back by pushing up on the slide stop lever. Visually and physically inspect the chamber to confirm it is empty. If you do not wish to leave the slide locked back, release the slide stop lever to let the slide go forward on an empty chamber.

Loaded Chamber Indicator

The Glock 43X has a loaded chamber indicator. It is a small raised portion of the extractor that protrudes slightly when a round is in the chamber. You can see and feel this indicator. However, always visually and physically verify chamber status rather than relying solely on the indicator.

Slide Lock / Slide Release

When the last round is fired, the follower in the magazine pushes up on the slide stop lever, locking the slide to the rear. To release the slide after inserting a fresh magazine, you can either press down on the slide stop lever or "slingshot" the slide by grasping the rear serrations and pulling rearward, then releasing. Both methods are acceptable. Many instructors prefer the slingshot method for consistency under stress.

05 Field Strip & Maintenance

Field Strip Procedure

Safety first. Remove all ammunition from your workspace. Remove the magazine. Lock the slide back and visually and physically confirm the chamber is empty. Let the slide go forward.

Field-Stripped G43X

From left: frame (receiver), barrel, recoil spring assembly, slide. The barrel's feed ramp and the slide rails are the highest-priority cleaning areas.

Point the pistol in a safe direction. With the magazine removed and chamber confirmed empty, pull the trigger to release the striker (dry fire). Grasp the slide and pull it rearward approximately 1/8 inch. While holding the slide in that position, pull down on both sides of the slide lock lever (the takedown tabs located on the frame above and forward of the trigger). While holding the takedown tabs down, push the slide forward off the frame.

Remove the recoil spring assembly from the slide by lifting it slightly and pulling it out. Remove the barrel from the slide by lifting the rear end and sliding it forward.

You now have four components: frame, slide, barrel, and recoil spring assembly. This is the maximum disassembly Glock intends for end users. Internal slide disassembly (striker, extractor, etc.) is considered armorer-level work.

Cleaning

Barrel. Run a bore brush or bore snake with CLP through the barrel to remove carbon fouling. Follow with clean patches until they come out clean. Scrub the exterior with a nylon brush, paying particular attention to the feed ramp. A clean feed ramp is critical for reliable feeding. Wipe dry and apply a thin film of oil.
Slide. Nylon brush to scrub carbon deposits from the breech face, extractor claw, and interior slide rails. Wipe down with a CLP-dampened cloth. Do not oil the striker channel. Any oil that enters the striker channel can slow the striker and cause light primer strikes or failures to fire.
Frame/Receiver. Brush debris from the frame rails, trigger mechanism area, and magazine well using a nylon brush. Wipe down with a clean cloth. The polymer frame does not require lubrication, but the metal frame rails do.
Recoil Spring Assembly. Wipe down with a lightly oiled cloth. Inspect for kinks, damage, or loss of tension.

Lubrication

Slide Rail & Barrel Lube Points

One drop in each slide rail groove, one on the barrel hood, one on the barrel lug. Wipe the recoil spring with an oily cloth.

The 3-2-1 Method

3 drops on the slide (2 rails + 1 hood), 2 on the barrel (hood + lug), 1 on the connector/trigger bar interface.

Glock pistols are designed to run with very little lubrication. Over-lubrication attracts debris and unburnt powder, forming sludge that can impair function. Six drops of oil total.

Never allow oil to enter the firing pin/striker channel. Oil in the striker channel can slow striker travel, causing light primer strikes and failures to fire. If you suspect oil has entered the channel, have it cleaned by a certified Glock armorer.

Reassembly

Insert the barrel into the slide, rear end slightly elevated, and push it forward into position. Insert the recoil spring assembly onto the barrel, seating the front end against the barrel's half-moon cutout and pressing the rear down into the slide. Align the slide onto the frame's rails and push it fully rearward until it clicks into place.

Perform a function check: rack the slide, pull the trigger (you should hear the striker click), rack the slide again, and verify that the trigger resets with a short forward movement and click. Verify the slide locks back on an empty magazine.

06 Concealed Carry & Holsters

The Glock 43X was purpose-built for concealed carry. Its 1.10-inch width and 6.50-inch length make it highly concealable, while the full-length grip provides significantly better purchase and control than the shorter G43.

Holster Selection

Kydex IWB Holster

Full trigger guard coverage with positive click retention. The 43X's 1.10" width makes it ideal for appendix or strong-side carry.

Light-Bearing IWB

If you mount a weapon light, you need a holster molded for that specific light + gun combination. The light housing becomes part of the retention geometry.

Because the Glock 43X has no manual safety, a proper holster is non-negotiable. The holster must fully cover the trigger guard to prevent any object from contacting the trigger. It must provide positive retention (audible click when holstered, resistance when drawing). It must be made of rigid material: Kydex, injection-molded polymer, or quality leather with a reinforced mouth. And it must be specific to the Glock 43X (or 43X/48 Slimline, which share the same frame).

If you mount a weapon light or optic, you will need a holster specifically designed to accommodate those accessories.

Carry Positions

Appendix IWB (AIWB). The most popular position for the 43X. The slim profile makes it very comfortable at the 12-1 o'clock position.
Strong-side IWB (3-4 o'clock). A traditional carry position. The 43X's full-length grip can print slightly more here than at appendix, but is still very concealable.
Pocket carry. The 43X is borderline for pocket carry. Its full-length grip makes it larger than ideal. The standard G43 is a better pocket gun.

Carry Condition

The standard and recommended carry condition for a Glock is "Condition One": a round in the chamber, full magazine seated, and the pistol holstered. The Safe Action system is specifically designed for this carry condition. Carrying with an empty chamber negates the primary advantage of a carry pistol. Immediate readiness.

07 Ammunition

The Glock 43X is chambered in 9x19mm Parabellum (9mm Luger). It will reliably feed standard-pressure (115 gr, 124 gr, 147 gr) and +P loads. Glock does not recommend +P+ ammunition, as it exceeds SAAMI pressure specifications and can accelerate wear.

Practice vs. Defensive

FMJ vs. Hollow Point

FMJ (left) for practice. Affordable, less barrel fouling. JHP (right) for carry. Expands on impact for effective energy transfer and reduced over-penetration.

Federal HST 124gr

One of the most widely recommended defensive 9mm loads. Consistent expansion, deep penetration through barriers, and excellent reliability in compact pistols.

Practice/Range. Full metal jacket (FMJ) rounds in 115 gr or 124 gr are the most common and affordable training ammunition. Brass-cased is preferred over steel-cased for reliability and less fouling.
Defensive/Carry. Jacketed hollow point (JHP) ammunition designed for self-defense. Popular choices include Federal HST (124 gr or 147 gr), Speer Gold Dot (124 gr), Hornady Critical Defense (115 gr), and Sig Sauer V-Crown (124 gr). Verify that your specific pistol reliably feeds your chosen defensive load by running at least 100-200 rounds through it before trusting it for carry.

Break-In Period

Glock does not specify a formal break-in period. However, running 200-500 rounds through a new pistol to verify function with your chosen ammunition and magazines is widely recommended.

08 Accessories & Upgrades

Sights

Tritium Night Sights

Trijicon HD XR. Orange front sight with tritium vial for visibility in all lighting conditions. No batteries. Tritium glows for 12+ years.

G43X MOS + Red Dot

The MOS slide accepts micro red dots like the Holosun 407K/507K without adapter plates. Adds roughly $100 to base price. Optic purchased separately.

Factory Glock sights are functional but basic. Upgrading to night sights (tritium) or fiber optic sights is one of the most popular first modifications. Options include Trijicon HD XR, AmeriGlo Bold/Hackathorn, and Night Fission Perfect Dot.

Shield Arms S15 Magazines

S15 Gen 3

15 rounds of 9mm, flush-fit, hardened steel with black nitrocarburized QPQ finish. Same external dimensions as the factory 10-round magazine.

S15 + 5 Extension

20 rounds total. Extends below the grip. Best suited for range, competition, or home defense rather than concealed carry.

The Shield Arms S15 is a flush-fitting, 15-round steel magazine that fits the Glock 43X and 48. It provides a 50% capacity increase in the same external footprint as the factory magazine.

Important: The S15 requires swapping the factory polymer magazine catch for a Shield Arms steel magazine catch. The steel S15 will wear down the factory polymer catch over time. Shield Arms also offers +2 and +5 magazine extensions, bringing capacity to 17 or 20 rounds respectively.

Weapon Lights

Streamlight TLR-7 Sub

~500 lumens, compact form factor, USB rechargeable. The most popular light for the railed 43X. Sits flush with the muzzle.

SureFire XSC

Ultra-compact at 350 lumens. Barely extends past the trigger guard. Premium aluminum build. The minimalist's choice.

Current-production 43X models include a Slimline accessory rail. A weapon light is strongly recommended for any defensive pistol. Target identification in low-light conditions is critical.

Optics (MOS Models)

The 43X MOS accepts micro red dot optics using the RMSc footprint. Popular choices include the Holosun 407K/507K, the Trijicon RMRcc, and the Shield RMSc. Adding a red dot improves target acquisition speed and accuracy. Co-witnessing with suppressor-height iron sights is recommended as a backup.

Trigger Upgrades

Aftermarket triggers and connectors can reduce pull weight, smooth the trigger stroke, and shorten reset. The Ghost Edge 3.5 lb connector is a popular drop-in option. Note that modifying the trigger on a carry gun is debated. Some instructors and legal experts advise against it for defensive firearms.

Other Common Upgrades

Extended magazine release. Easier to reach for faster reloads.
Extended slide stop/release. Easier to manipulate with the thumb.
Magwell. Funnels the magazine into the grip for faster reloads.
Grip enhancements. Talon Grips, Hogue grip sleeves, or stippling improve purchase.
Threaded barrel. Allows attachment of a compensator or suppressor.
09 Troubleshooting
Failure to feed (FTF). Most commonly caused by a dirty feed ramp, weak magazine spring, riding the slide forward, or incompatible bullet profile. Clean the feed ramp, use quality magazines, and rack the slide assertively.
Failure to eject / stovepipe (FTE). Often caused by limp-wristing, a dirty/worn extractor, or underpowered ammunition. Verify grip technique first. If persistent, inspect the extractor.
Failure to fire / light primer strike. Check ammunition first. If multiple rounds fail, the striker spring may be weak or oil may have entered the striker channel. Have a certified armorer inspect the striker assembly.
Slide difficult to rack or gritty. Clean and lubricate the slide rails. Inspect for burrs or debris. New pistols may feel stiff until broken in.
Magazine won't seat or drop free. Ensure correct magazine type (OEM 43X/48 or S15 with correct mag catch). Check for debris in the magazine well.
10 Safe Storage

When not on your person, the Glock 43X should be stored securely to prevent unauthorized access, particularly by children.

Quick-Access Handgun Safe

Biometric, keypad, or Bluetooth access. Mounts to a nightstand, shelf, or inside a drawer. The Vaultek VT20i is a popular choice for the 43X.

Glock Cable Lock

Included with every new Glock. Threads through the magazine well and ejection port. A minimum-viable option. Not a substitute for a locked safe when children are present.

For longer-term storage when the pistol is not part of your carry rotation, a full-size gun safe or cabinet is appropriate. Apply a thin coat of oil to all metal surfaces, store in a low-humidity environment, and periodically inspect for corrosion.

11 Oklahoma Carry Context
This is not legal advice. Consult Oklahoma statutes and/or an attorney for current, definitive guidance. These provisions can and do change.
Constitutional (permitless) carry. Oklahoma allows persons 21 and older (18+ for military) who are legally eligible to possess a firearm to carry concealed or open without a permit, effective November 1, 2019.
Vehicle transport. A loaded, concealed handgun may be carried in a vehicle by anyone legally eligible to possess it.
SDA license. Oklahoma's Self-Defense Act license is optional but provides reciprocity with other states, may expedite NICS checks, and serves as proof of training.
Prohibited locations. Carry is prohibited in certain locations regardless of permit status, including government buildings (with posted signage), schools, courthouses, and private property with proper signage per Oklahoma statute.
Employer/workplace. Oklahoma law allows employees to store firearms locked in their vehicles on employer property, but employers can prohibit carry inside the workplace.
12 Training

Owning a Glock 43X for defensive purposes carries an obligation to train with it.

Complete a basic handgun safety and fundamentals course if you have not already.
Attend a concealed carry / defensive handgun course from a reputable instructor.
Practice regularly: dry-fire drills at home (confirm the pistol is unloaded) for trigger control, draw stroke, and sight alignment. Live-fire range sessions at least monthly.
Practice drawing from your actual carry holster and clothing at the range (where permitted).
Train malfunction clearances: tap-rack-bang for failures to fire, lock-strip-rack-reload for double feeds.
Consider an advanced course covering low-light shooting, shooting on the move, and use of cover.
