Chosen Fatherhood

A legacy of chosen family, three generations deep

My dad was adopted. His biological father wasn't around, and my grandfather David stepped in and claimed him as his own. My dad never forgot what that meant. He raised me the way he was raised; chosen, on purpose, without conditions.

I don't think I understood the full shape of that until I had children of my own.

When I met Alli, I didn't just fall in love with her. I fell in love with Norah and Nehemiah too. From the start, part of what I was stepping into was the work of showing two kids what safety and consistency and steady love actually look like, day after day, until they believe it. I showed up. I answered when they came to me for comfort or for guidance. They started calling me Dad. In my heart they were mine long before any court said so.

I tried to love them the way each of them needed, not as a pair but as two distinct people. Norah opened her big heart to me without hesitation, the way she opens it to everyone; she made room for me before I had done anything to earn it. Nehemiah let me in more like he builds, carefully and on his own terms, until the two of us were spending hours building worlds together, talking about God, learning side by side. I paid close attention to who each of them was, and I loved that person specifically.

Norah used to ask me, more than once, when she and her brother would finally share the same last name as Alli and me. She wanted to belong, visibly and permanently, to the family she already lived inside of. It mattered to her in a way that was both simple and profound. It mattered to me just as much.

The night before it became official, I couldn't sleep. At nearly three in the morning I sat down and wrote each of them a letter. Not one letter to both of them; two separate letters, because they are two separate people, and each of them deserved their own, one that reflected back exactly who I saw when I looked at them.

In October 2025 the adoption was finalized. By law and by name, they became my children; which is to say, the paperwork finally caught up to the truth.
